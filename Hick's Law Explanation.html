Hick’s Law (also known as Hick–Hyman Law) is a principle in psychology and user experience (UX) design that states:

The more choices a person has, the longer it will take them to make a decision.

💡 Key Idea:

When users are presented with more options, their decision time increases logarithmically — not linearly — meaning each additional option adds slightly less extra time, but still increases the total.

🔢 Formula:

T=a+b⋅log⁡2(n+1)T = a + b \cdot \log_2(n + 1)T=a+b⋅log2​(n+1)
Where:

  • TTT = reaction time

  • aaa and bbb = constants based on task and individual

  • nnn = number of choices

🎯 Real-World Examples:

  • Web design: Too many menu items or buttons can overwhelm users.

  • Remote controls: More buttons = more time to find the one you want.

  • Forms: Fewer options in a dropdown or clearer categories = faster completion.

✅ UX Tips Based on Hick’s Law:

  • Limit choices to essential options.

  • Group related options.

  • Use progressive disclosure (show details only when needed).

  • Prioritize default or recommended actions.

Slide 3: Why It Matters in UX

  • Users want fast and easy experiences.

  • Hick’s Law helps designers create cleaner, quicker interfaces.

Slide 4: History of Hick’s Law

  • First tested in psychology labs in 1952.

  • Hick and Hyman timed how long it took people to choose from different numbers of options.

  • Result: More options = slower decisions.

  • Today, it’s used to design websites, apps, remotes, and menus.
